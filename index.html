The Observer
& His Sky

I'm a Distinguished Engineer working at the intersection of AI/ML and LLM-augmented systems. A year ago I discovered that the same curiosity that drives me to build things by day could be pointed upward at night.

Stargazing from Bengaluru — a city of 12 million people and considerable light pollution — turns out to be both a constraint and a creative challenge. What's possible under these skies genuinely surprised me.

Everything here was captured afocally: iPhone 16 Plus held to the eyepiece. No dedicated astronomy camera, no image stacking, no dark sky site. Just patience, a steady mount, and clear air.

Bengaluru · 12.97° N, 77.59° E · ~920m elevation

Notes on Technique

Method

Afocal Photography

The iPhone 16 Plus is held or clamped directly to the telescope eyepiece — no adapters, no dedicated astrocamera. The phone's computational photography stack does the rest. Surprisingly effective for bright objects.

TechniqueAfocal
FormatProRAW + HEIC
ProcessingLightroom Mobile
The Challenge

Urban Skies

Bengaluru sits at Bortle Class 7 — suburban/urban with a noticeably bright horizon glow. Deep sky objects require patience. But planets, the Moon, and bright stars are entirely viable targets.

Bortle Class7 · Urban
Best targetsMoon, planets, bright stars
Best monthsNov – Mar
The Platform

Equatorial Tracking

The motorised equatorial platform compensates for Earth's rotation, keeping objects stationary in the eyepiece long enough for the iPhone to expose properly without trailing.

Tracking rateSidereal
Key benefitNo star trailing
Runtime~60 min per reset
